🎓 J-1 Visa Covers Many Different People — Same Tax Rules

J-1 covers students, scholars, researchers, teachers, au pairs, summer work travel participants, trainees and more. All share the same core tax obligation: Form 1040-NR for US income, FICA exemption in most cases, and Form 8843 regardless of income.

J-1 Categories

Which J-1 Category Are You?

Tax rules are similar across all J-1 categories — the differences are mainly in treaty benefits and duration of exemption.

🎓
Student / Scholar
F-1 equivalent for exchange programs. Nonresident alien for first 2 years. Must file 1040-NR and Form 8843. Treaty benefits available for many countries.
🏠
Au Pair
Working in a US family home. Wages are taxable US income. FICA exempt as J-1 nonresident. Must file 1040-NR. No standard deduction.
🔬
Researcher / Professor
Attached to US university or institution. Same rules as scholars. Many countries have treaty benefits covering research income.
☀️
Summer Work Travel
J-1 summer program workers — FICA exempt, 1040-NR required. Many workers are owed refunds from withheld federal income tax.
⚠️
After 2 Years — Check Status
J-1 teachers and researchers are nonresident for first 2 years. After 2 years, apply the Substantial Presence Test to determine resident or nonresident status.
⚠️
J-2 Dependents
J-2 spouses and children also must file Form 8843 if present in the US — even with no income.
What to File

J-1 Tax Forms — What You Need

📋
Form 1040-NR
Main tax return for J-1 nonresident aliens with US income. Required for all J-1 holders with wages, stipends or other US-source income.
📋
Form 8843
Required for ALL J-1 holders in the US during 2025 — even with zero income. Establishes nonresident alien exempt status.
📋
Schedule OI
Other Information schedule — part of 1040-NR. Required for all filers. Reports visa type, treaty claims and days in the US.
📋
W-2 or 1042-S
W-2 for wages from US employers. Form 1042-S for scholarship or fellowship income and treaty-exempt wages.

FAQ

J-1 Tax FAQ

Am I FICA exempt as a J-1 visa holder?
Yes — J-1 nonresident aliens are exempt from Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) taxes. This applies to all J-1 categories including au pairs, summer workers, scholars and researchers, as long as you are in nonresident alien status. If your employer withheld FICA, file IRS Form 843 to recover it.
J-1 au pairs — do I pay tax on my stipend?
Yes — au pair stipends are taxable wages subject to US federal income tax. You are not FICA exempt from income tax (only from Social Security and Medicare). Your host family may or may not withhold federal income tax. If not withheld, you may owe it when you file. File Form 1040-NR by April 15.
Can I claim the standard deduction on my J-1 return?
Generally no — nonresident aliens cannot claim the standard deduction. However, residents of India can claim the same standard deduction as US citizens under the US-India treaty. All nonresidents can claim itemized deductions on Schedule A of Form 1040-NR.
I did summer work travel and left the US — do I still need to file?
Yes — if you earned any wages in the US during the summer, you must file Form 1040-NR for that income. You may also be owed a refund of withheld federal income tax. File by April 15 or June 15 if you had no withholding. e-file.com handles 1040-NR from outside the US.

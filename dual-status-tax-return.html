🔄 Changing Visas Mid-Year Creates Dual Status — Most People Don't Know This

If you changed from F-1 (nonresident) to H-1B and became a US tax resident during 2025, you are a dual-status alien. Your return covers two periods with different rules — the nonresident period and the resident period. This is one of the most complex individual tax situations.

What is Dual Status

Dual Status Alien — The Basics

📅
Two Tax Periods in One Year
Nonresident period: taxed only on US-source income. Resident period: taxed on worldwide income. Each period has different rules, deductions and rates.
📋
Form 1040 as Main Return
Use Form 1040 as your main return for the resident period. Attach Form 1040-NR (or a statement) for the nonresident period.
❌
Cannot File Jointly (Usually)
Dual-status aliens generally cannot file Married Filing Jointly. Exception: make a first-year choice election to be treated as resident for the full year — complex but beneficial for some.
❌
No Standard Deduction
Dual-status aliens cannot claim the standard deduction for the nonresident period. Itemized deductions only for that period — often resulting in higher tax.
⚠️
F-1 to H-1B — Common Scenario
F-1 students who transition to H-1B on October 1 and pass the Substantial Presence Test become dual-status. Nonresident Jan 1–Sep 30, resident Oct 1–Dec 31.
✅
May Elect Full-Year Resident
If married, you and your spouse may elect to be treated as US residents for the entire year — simpler filing, standard deduction, joint return. File a statement with Form 1040.
Common Scenarios

When You Become Dual Status

🎓→💼
F-1 OPT to H-1B Oct 1
Most common. H-1B starts Oct 1. If you pass the SPT for the year, you are nonresident Jan–Sep, resident Oct–Dec. Dual-status return required.
🛫
Arrived Mid-Year
If you arrived in the US mid-year and met the SPT, you are nonresident before arrival and resident after. File dual-status for that year.
🛫
Left the US Mid-Year
If you left permanently or became nonresident after being a resident, you are dual-status. File Form 1040-C (departure permit) before leaving.
✅
Election to Be Full-Year Resident
Married couples can elect full-year resident status on a jointly filed 1040. Simpler, allows standard deduction, but means worldwide income taxed for full year.

FAQ

Dual Status FAQ

I changed from F-1 to H-1B on October 1 — do I file 1040 or 1040-NR?
You likely file a dual-status return. Use Form 1040 as the main return for October–December (resident period) and attach Form 1040-NR (or equivalent statement) for January–September (nonresident period). The exact treatment depends on whether you pass the Substantial Presence Test for the full year.
Is it better to elect full-year resident status?
It depends on your situation. Full-year resident status: simpler filing, standard deduction, can file jointly with spouse, but worldwide income taxed for the full year. Dual-status: more complex, no standard deduction for nonresident period, but only US-source income taxed during nonresident period. For most F-1 to H-1B transitions, a tax professional should evaluate both options.
Can I claim the F-1 FICA exemption for the period I was on OPT?
Yes — for the months you were in valid F-1/OPT status as a nonresident alien, you are FICA exempt. For the months you were in H-1B status as a resident alien, FICA applies. Your employer should adjust withholding accordingly. If FICA was withheld during OPT, file Form 843 to recover it.
